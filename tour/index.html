We still need to figure out how exactly the food in the fridge is getting detected.
Most object classifiers are quite well-trained on state-of-the-art convolutional neural networks like;

AlexNet, VGG, ResNet, DenseNet, Inceptionv3, GoogLeNet, MobileNet, EfficientNet

I picked the highlighted ones to train our data because of the speed of training and resources I had available to us.
Where is the data?

Out of 8 publicly available datasets on grocery items, I found one most suitable to the application:

Freiburg Groceries Dataset by University of Freiburg
It has 25 classes and ~5000 images;
Beans, Cake, Candy, Cereal, Chips, Chocolate, Coffee, Corn, Fish, Flour
Honey, Jam, Juice, Milk, Nuts, Oil, Pasta, Rice, Soda, Spices, Sugar, Tea, Tomato Sauce, Vinegar, Water
So far VGG16 is the fastest and least resource consuming model performing at 76.16% accuracy.

I plan to finetune it and play around with the learning rate scheduler more.
But ingredients in the fridge are seldom organized and spaced out.

We need to detect and segment items/ingredients before we can classify them.
